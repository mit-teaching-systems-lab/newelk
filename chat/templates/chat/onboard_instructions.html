In ELK, the third role is not one that would actually exist in a real-life conversation between a student and their teacher. This role, “the coder,” does not participate in the conversation, but rather assigns “codes,” or message types, to the teacher’s messages. The assigned codes will be used to give feedback to the teacher. Today, you will look at a few transcripts of conversations and code the teacher’s messages. In all transcripts, T is the teacher and S is the student, independent of their names. The codes and definitions are provided below.



Each message can have any number of codes (0-5) attached to it.
  • Priming - These are meta-messages set the context for the conversation. They might appear at the beginning of the conversation or later to bring the conversation back to the topic/goal. Although useful, Priming messages should be used in moderation. Some examples of Priming messages are:
    • Hi. Is it okay if I ask you a few questions about circles?
    • [after a student asks, “Is that right?”] I will tell you all about that during class today, but for now I just want to understand your ideas.
  • Eliciting - These are questions that introduce a new topic or broaden the discussion (as opposed to follow-up questions that go deeper). For example, if the teacher is asking the student about circles, initial questions about the area or circumference would be Eliciting messages. Some examples of Eliciting messages are:
    • What do you know about the area of a circle?
    • Tell me what you remember about sentence structure.
  • Probing - These are follow-up questions that go deeper into what the student thinks. It is often impossible to tell the difference between Eliciting and Probing messages without context. Some examples of Probing messages in context are:
    • From a conversation about circles:
      • Teacher: What do you know about the circumference of a circle? Eliciting
      • Student: The circumference of a circle in 2𝜋r.
      • Teacher: What’s the circumference of a circle with diameter 6? Probing
    • From a conversation about sentence structure:
      • Teacher: What do you know about word order? Eliciting
      • Student: The noun comes before the verb.
      • Teacher: Is this always true? Or are there ever nouns after verbs in a sentence? Probing
  • Evaluating - These messages tell the student if they were right or wrong, either explicitly or implicitly. They often lead to Telling messages and distract the student from the goal of figuring out their preconceptions. Some examples of Evaluating messages are:
    • Nice!
    • Not quite.
    • That’s right.
    • You’re confusing the radius with the diameter.
  • Telling - These are messages in which the teacher explains what’s true. While important during instruction, they are distracting while figuring out what the student already knew or believed. Some examples of Telling messages are:
    • The circumference of a circle in 2𝜋r.
    • You’re confusing the radius with the diameter. Evaluating + Telling

Remember that each message can have anywhere between none to all the codes. For example, “That’s right! What do you remember about the area of a circle?” is both Evaluating because of the first part and Eliciting because of the second part.
